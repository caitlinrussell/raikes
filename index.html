Agile Development

In The Startup World

Caitlin Bales / @caitlinabales

1. Be Lazy

  • It already exists
  • Use plugins, frameworks, software packages
  • Be afraid of beta
  • Resist over-architecting
  • Be creative!
  • There’s something already out there for that
  • Plugins, frameworks, etc.
  • You don’t have to hide your face in shame if you use a Wordpress site
  • Research, but don’t paralyze yourself with options. Look at popular options that have a lot of support and seem like they’ll be around in 5 years
  • Don’t use anything in beta
  • Look for new ways to use software as it may not quite have been intended

2. Schedule Wisely

  • Plan your release dates around your customers
  • Give your team time to provide assets & testing
  • Know thyself
  • Expect failure
  • You may not have a formal release date, so you’ll have to pick one yourself
  • Is there a good time of year to release something to your customer? What does the purchasing cycle look like? 15 min? 1 month? A quarter? When will your sales and marketing teams be able to sell it?
  • I use an Excel spreadsheet with hours for everything, you could use Scrum style hours, or you could make some gross Gantt chart. Know what your tendency is (over/under time estimates) and always be correcting
  • Give yourself time for things to go wrong and to fix them! If it’s a brand new product you can do more of this after release, if you’re revamping existing work, do as much testing and prevention as possible beforehand

3. Iterate

  • The product will change
  • The customer might change
  • Choose the right fidelity of prototyping for your team
  • Depending on how far out your release is, it may not make sense to prototype
  • Weigh the cost of prototyping vs. rewriting existing code

4. Make Something Awesome

  • Leverage what's already out there
  • Beware the Unknown
  • Ship early, ship often
  • Get the language right
  • Nice-to-Haves are nice to have
  • Leverage what’s already out there
  • Start at the foundation just as you would on a big project, but then create features at a time
  • Create testable units for your team to review
  • Show early and often
  • Refactor – chances are the vocabulary will change. “Sampling program” vs. “campaign” vs. “listing” vs. “product profile”. It’s really important to have a common vocabulary between the software, the company, and the customer
  • Add in little nice-to-haves once in a while
  • These often are the most-loved features even though they don’t necessarily “do” anything. Think (non-obnoxious) hover effects, inline editing w/ AJAX, etc
  • A lot of these come for free with a library and a few lines of code

5. Say Yes

  • Make yourself more approachable
  • Help them ask you questions
  • When you say "no", mean it
  • Even if a programmer is business savvy, the technicality of the domain can spook non-techies. Saying yes as much as possible makes your work more approachable
  • Help them ask critical questions when testing your work. Non-programmers are great at picking up on grammatical mistakes, UI hiccups, and bumpy workflows, but they sometimes need an outline of how to bring that up to you
  • This also gives you leverage when you say no to something; you really mean it!

6. Find a Business Friend

Do you know how to:

  • Write a press release
  • Launch a marketing campaign
  • Pitch to investors
  • Babysit investors
  • Write a sales pitch
  • Create financial forecasts

Bulu Marketplace

  • Built a platform to share existing data with our customers
  • Shared database with our sampling program to share information quickly and easily
  • Used Magento, an e-commerce platform, to develop a partner portal – a lot of these are the same!
  • Design changed twice in the 3 week dev time
  • Created a marketplace for buyers and sellers to connect
  • Changed the UI – again
  • Changed the name but reused existing Insights code
  • Store connection proved even more important than in Insights – brands who worked with us could migrate their data to Insights and our staff could incorporate new brands who had marketplace profiles even more rapidly than on BuluBox.com
  • 6 week dev time corresponded with new year’s budgeting
  • Releasing seller side first allowed us to get brands signed up before advertising to buyers
